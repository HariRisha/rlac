# Crash Course in AI-Generated Images

## Event Details:
Date: Thursday, February 19th, 2026
Time: 11am - 12 pm

## Response

Artificial Intelligence is rapidly transforming creative fields, especially those in the area of visual content production. We are seeing more and more AI generated images and videos being used not just on social media and informal settings, but also in schools, conferences and other formal settings. That’s why I believe it is important that we adapt to these changes and learn more about what they are, how they are done, and what’s the best and correct way to use them. 

A great opportunity to learn further about these topics was the Crash Course in AI-Generated Images that I participated in. The course was more focused on open discussion and personal experience rather than just lectures like the usual courses. We were shown some AI and some non-AI generated images and were asked to try to guess which ones were AI and which were not. In addition to this we also had the opportunity to look at the progression of AI models through different outputs with the same prompts over the years and see what had changed and to what degree. It was really interesting to see how easy it was to spot AI generated images at the beginning of “GPT” era and how progressively it became harder and harder over the years to the point where guessing what images were AI generated was extremely difficult. The instructors were holding the discussions and were also trying to guide us to pick out which parts of the images we should pay more attention to when we are trying to catch AI generation. The instructors had spent months researching AI image generation and had experience in prompt writing and image identification so it was great to hear from someone with extensive experience in the topic. One thing I noticed, though, is that the students had more experience for identifying AI images and writing better prompts in comparison to professors and staff who struggled much more with such. The AI-s we focused on were Chat GPT and Perplexity and at the end we got to see an example from Claude. This was particularly useful as we had an opportunity to see which AI worked best for image generation and from what we could see Claude was very much ahead of the rest, with GPT producing the worst outputs. 

Given the fact that we often talk about LLM-s and their usage and capabilities in class I feel like getting a deeper understanding of their image production proved to be a great base for the course and especially response  1 where we had the LLM-s interpret the graphs as images. I feel like the crash course gave me a glimpse at the different side of working with AI and imaging where instead of having them interpret the images we had them create some. Even though the concepts seem completely different, personally I believe that to properly understand how an AI is able to “read” images, we need to understand how it creates them as this gives us deeper understanding on its way of thinking, strengths and weaknesses. Building on this, one of the most important things that I took away from the course was the degree to which the user has control over the final output. For example, making slight changes to the prompt in terms of style and detail can greatly impact the final output. This has also made me realize that using AI is not a one-way process, rather it is a multi part collaboration between the user and the machine where the user is actively involved in the output. Another important point that was emphasized during the course was the limitations of AI. Even though AI can produce images that are highly realistic and detailed, there are still some limitations in terms of hands, text, and logical consistency in complex scenes. I believe this is important to keep in mind while using AI-generated images in academic and professional settings. This also relates to some of the points that have been emphasized in the course regarding interpretation and reliability. Just as AI can be used to misinterpret facts and produce misleading images and graphs, it can also be used to produce images that are highly realistic and detailed but not based in reality.

Looking ahead, I believe that the knowledge gained from the crash course will greatly help me with future work for my capstone and my career. As I mentioned earlier, artificial image generation has progressed greatly and will continue to, at an even bigger pace, in the future and I strongly believe that we need to be prepared for that future and know how to use the service and how to recognise when someone else is using it. I believe that this knowledge can help me in creating images that assist me in conceptualizing ideas for my capstone or creating proper visuals for my presentations. An important note for the future is that even though this course has given me important knowledge on how to use AI for image generation properly it has also left us with an important note on ethical considerations for environmental destruction that LLM-s cause. We mentioned how excessive use of AI, especially on image generation, needed more electricity and more server space and as such motivated the corporations to expand their facilities, destroying the environment in addition to using large amounts of water for cooling. This also explains why the course was designed as a conversation-based session rather than a hands-on workshop, as the instructors prioritized examining pre-generated outputs over requiring us to generate new content through intensive AI prompts.
